ENERGY SOURCES

We will look at our strategy when it comes to alternative fuels, but before that, you need a basic understanding of the different energy sources that are considered in the truck industry.

Click on each energy type to learn more about it!

DIESEL

Diesel is a liquid fossil fuel produced by distilling crude oil. It is one of the most energy dense fuels available today, and by far the most widely used in all regions of the world for various heavy vehicle types.

Burning diesel leads to emissions of particles and NOx. Through the use of advanced combustion and post-treatment technologies, these emissions have been substantially reduced. Burning diesel also produces CO2. For each litre of diesel used, 2.6 kg of CO2 is emitted. This figure cannot be altered, it is directly related to the chemical reaction of burning diesel.

BIODIESEL

Biodiesel is diesel fuel produced by refining vegetal oil or animal fat, for example rapeseed or sunflower seeds. Biodiesel can be blended with diesel in various proportions. Most common is a 30% blend, called B30, or pure biodiesel, called B100. In Europe today, standard diesel is blended with 7 % biodiesel and is called B7. Biodiesel has the same attributes as diesel in terms of energy density, particle and NOx emissions as well as tailpipe emissions of CO2, but due to specific chemical properties, blends exceeding 7% biodiesel cannot be used in standard engines and specific engine variants are thus required to use this fuel.

From a well-to-wheel perspective, biodiesel is more environmentally friendly. Due to it being produced from vegetal oil or animal fat, the CO2 emissions are about 60 % lower than regular diesel in a well-to-wheel perspective if B100 is used.

xTL

Synthetic paraffinic fuels, or xTLs, are synthetic diesel fuels. They are a new generation of cleaner transport fuels that can be made of a variety of feedstock, such as biomass (BTL-Biomass To Liquid), natural gas (GTL-Gas To Liquid) or coal (CTL-Coal To Liquid). The feedstock is made into a liquid fuel. The most well-known example is HVO (Hydrogenated Vegetable Oil), which is a type of BTL.

xTLs are very clean fuels from a chemical standpoint and all Renault Trucks Euro VI engines are compatible with xTL fuels.

Depending on feedstock, the greenhouse gas emissions from synthetic fuels are reduced by up to 90 % from a well-to-wheel perspective. When it comes to polluting emissions such as particles and NOx they are on par with emissions from regular diesel.

NATURAL GAS

Natural gas is mainly made up of methane. It is a fossil fuel that is formed underground as other hydrocarbon fuels. There are two different forms of natural gas used for trucks, compressed natural gas (CNG) and liquefied natural gas (LNG). Natural gas is an established fuel type in the truck industry, but with varying infrastructural support.

Methane gas itself is a potent greenhouse gas. However, when combusted, CO2 emissions from natural gas are in general about 5 % to 15 % lower from a well-to-wheel perspective compared to diesel. Particle emissions are considerably decreased, while NOx emissions are on par with emissions for a Euro VI certified diesel engine.

BIOGAS

Biogas is a renewable fuel which mainly consists of methane gas, similar to natural gas. There are two different forms of biogas used for trucks, compressed biogas (Bio-CNG or CBG) and liquefied biogas gas (Bio-LNG or LBG). Biogas can be used in natural gas engines, and is therefore an established fuel type in the truck industry, but with varying infrastructural support.

Methane gas itself is a potent greenhouse gas. However, when combusted, CO2 emissions from natural gas are in general up to 70 % lower from a well-to-wheel perspective compared to diesel. Particle emissions are considerably decreased, while NOx emissions are on par with emissions for a Euro VI certified diesel engine.

HYDROGEN

Hydrogen generates electrical power in a fuel cell. It is produced from diverse domestic resources like natural gas, coal, solar energy, wind and biomass. Hydrogen is currently not widely utilized in the truck industry, but shows strong potential in the long run.

Hydrogen enables zero tailpipe emissions. The well-to-wheel CO2 emissions are dependent on how the hydrogen was produced.

ELECTRICITY

Electricity can be produced from a wide variety of energy sources, both fossil fuels like oil but also renewable fuels like wind. The electric energy is then stored in a lithium-ion battery in the truck. It is currently not widely utilized in the truck industry, but is believed to be the future energy solution for transport. Recharging of the truck is done using the ordinary electric grid.

Electricity enables zero tailpipe emissions. The well-to-wheel CO2 emissions are dependent on how the electricity was produced.
